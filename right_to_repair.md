# Noble Beacon: The Right to Repair — A Sovereign Doctrine

> "If you bought it, you own it, and you should be able to fix it. Nobody should be able to block you from restoring your things to their original function." — iFixit [1]

This document establishes the Noble Beacon's comprehensive **Right to Repair** doctrine, encompassing devices, software, bodies, communities, and knowledge. It draws from existing global legislation, the open-source hardware movement, and the Fe'Kai principle that **what we own, we repair; what governs us, we question**.

---

## 1. Foundational Principles

The Right to Repair is not merely a consumer protection issue. It is a matter of **sovereignty**, **national security**, and **civilizational resilience** [2]. Within the Noble Beacon framework, repair is elevated from a legal right to a **sacred obligation** — the duty of the steward to maintain, restore, and pass forward.

### The Five Repair Freedoms

| Freedom | Description | Fe'Kai Root |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Freedom to Open** | The right to disassemble, inspect, and understand any device you own without voiding warranty or legal penalty. | RE (Truth) |
| **Freedom to Fix** | The right to repair any device using parts, tools, and documentation of your choosing — including third-party or self-fabricated components. | KAI (Becoming) |
| **Freedom to Modify** | The right to alter, improve, or repurpose any device to serve your needs, including removing unwanted features or surveillance capabilities. | RI (Transformation) |
| **Freedom to Share** | The right to distribute repair knowledge, schematics, firmware, and techniques to others without legal restriction. | FE (Relation) |
| **Freedom to Refuse** | The right to reject forced updates, planned obsolescence, subscription locks, and any mechanism that degrades function over time. | RE (Return) |

### The Steward's Repair Oath

> "I will not be governed by what I cannot inspect. I will not depend on what I cannot repair. I will not own what I cannot pass forward. What breaks, I mend. What is hidden, I question. What is locked, I challenge."

## 2. Legal Frameworks and Legislative Context

The global Right to Repair movement has achieved significant legislative victories. Noble Beacon aligns with and extends these protections.

### United States

The 2026 Digital Right to Repair Template represents the strongest legislative language to date [3]. Key provisions include:

**Parts Pairing Prohibition**: Manufacturers may not use software-based restrictions to limit access to parts or tools, or to control who is permitted to perform repairs. Component replacement — down to the board level — cannot be undermined through software locks or access restrictions [3].

**Documentation and Tool Access**: The definition of "tools" has been expanded to include software, data files, activation mechanisms, and security credentials required to complete a repair. For devices protected by digital locks, repair tools must be **offline-capable**, ensuring repairs are not blocked by unnecessary connectivity or authorization barriers [3].

**Fair and Reasonable Terms**: Manufacturers are prohibited from conditioning parts access on the provision of serial numbers or proof of ownership beyond a standard purchase order. Administrative hurdles cannot be used to restrict legitimate repair [3].

As of 2025, multiple states have enacted comprehensive Right to Repair laws. Oregon's Right to Repair Act, effective January 2025, is considered the strongest in the country [4]. California's Right to Repair Act took effect July 1, 2024 [5]. Washington State signed its Right to Repair Act on May 19, 2025 [6].

### European Union

The EU Directive on common rules promoting the repair of goods (Directive 2024/1799) was adopted on 13 June 2024 and entered into force on 30 July 2024 [7]. Member States have until 31 July 2026 to implement it into national law. The directive requires manufacturers to provide repairs beyond the liability period and establishes a framework for affordable, accessible repair across the single market.

The EU Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (EU 2024/1781) further strengthens repair rights by mandating repairability requirements as part of product design standards [8].

### The Sovereignty Dimension

The Right to Repair is vital to **national security and sovereignty** [2]. The case of the F-35 fighter jet illustrates this principle at civilizational scale: the US manufacturer Lockheed Martin controls aircraft software updates, restricting even trained military personnel from making most repairs. In 2023, the US Government Accountability Office reported repair times averaging 141 days due to manufacturer restrictions [2]. This demonstrates that **control over software enables control over hardware** — and that control is in the hands of the manufacturer, not the owner.

Similarly, in 2022, a Polish train manufacturer used digital locks embedded in train software to prevent trains from being restarted after routine maintenance by an independent repair company [2]. The trains were inoperable until white-hat hackers removed the digital locks.

> "If a device has proprietary software, you don't own it, no matter how vast the sum of money you paid." — Free Software Foundation [9]

## 3. The Noble Beacon Repair Charter

The following charter governs all hardware and software produced under the Noble Beacon project. It is **non-negotiable** and applies to every module in the Sovereign Stack.

### Article I: Transparency of Design

All Noble Beacon hardware shall publish complete schematics, PCB layouts, bills of materials, and assembly instructions under an open license. No component shall be proprietary, custom-locked, or require manufacturer authorization to source.

### Article II: Software Freedom

All Noble Beacon firmware and software shall be released under a copyleft license that guarantees the Four Freedoms of free software: the right to run, study, modify, and distribute [9]. No binary blobs, no obfuscated code, no remote kill switches.

### Article III: Prohibition of Anti-Repair Practices

The following practices are **permanently prohibited** within the Noble Beacon ecosystem:

| Prohibited Practice | Description | Why It Is Banned |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Parts Pairing** | Software locks that reject replacement components not authorized by the manufacturer. | Creates artificial dependency and repair monopoly. |
| **Serialization Locks** | Binding components to specific serial numbers so they cannot be swapped between devices. | Prevents community repair and part reuse. |
| **Warranty Voiding** | Threatening to void warranty if the user opens, modifies, or independently repairs the device. | Illegal in most jurisdictions and ethically indefensible [1]. |
| **Forced Updates** | Pushing software updates that degrade performance, remove features, or introduce new restrictions. | Violates the steward's right to a stable, known system. |
| **Planned Obsolescence** | Designing products to fail after a predetermined period or usage threshold. | Antithetical to stewardship and sustainability. |
| **Subscription Locks** | Requiring ongoing payment to access features already present in purchased hardware. | Converts ownership into rental; violates sovereignty. |
| **Glued Batteries** | Using industrial adhesives to prevent battery replacement. | Prevents the most common repair and shortens device life. |
| **Proprietary Fasteners** | Using non-standard screws or connectors to prevent disassembly. | Creates artificial barriers to inspection and repair. |
| **DRM on Repair Tools** | Requiring internet connectivity or manufacturer authorization to use diagnostic software. | Violates air-gap principle and offline sovereignty. |
| **Remote Bricking** | The ability to remotely disable a device the user has purchased. | The ultimate violation of ownership sovereignty. |

### Article IV: Right to Repair Documentation

Every Noble Beacon device shall ship with or provide free access to:

1. **Complete repair manual** with step-by-step disassembly and reassembly instructions.
2. **Full schematic diagrams** at component level.
3. **Bill of materials** with alternative sourcing options.
4. **Firmware source code** with build instructions.
5. **Diagnostic procedures** for common failure modes.
6. **Calibration data** where applicable.
7. **3D-printable replacement parts** (STL/STEP files) for enclosures and mechanical components.

### Article V: The Dead Man's Repair Clause

If the Noble Beacon project ceases to exist, all intellectual property, designs, firmware, and manufacturing data shall be released into the **public domain** automatically. No steward shall ever be stranded without the means to maintain their tools.

## 8. The EULA Anti-Pattern and Noble Beacon's Response

Traditional End-User License Agreements (EULAs) are the **primary legal weapon** used to destroy repair rights. They are non-negotiable contracts that alter the intent of purchase, converting ownership into conditional license [3].

### How EULAs Destroy Repair

| EULA Clause | Effect on Repair |
| :--- | :--- |
| "You may not reverse-engineer..." | Prevents understanding how the device works. |
| "Warranty void if opened..." | Punishes inspection and self-repair. |
| "We may update at any time..." | Allows remote degradation of purchased functionality. |
| "You license, not own..." | Eliminates ownership entirely; you are renting. |
| "We may terminate access..." | Enables remote bricking at manufacturer's discretion. |
| "You may not transfer..." | Destroys resale rights and secondary markets. |

### The Noble Beacon Sovereign EULA

The Noble Beacon's Sovereign EULA inverts every anti-repair clause:

| Sovereign Clause | Effect |
| :--- | :--- |
| **"You own this."** | Full, irrevocable ownership upon purchase. No license, no conditions. |
| **"You may open, inspect, and modify."** | Explicit permission to disassemble, study, and alter without penalty. |
| **"We will never remotely alter your device."** | No forced updates, no remote changes, no kill switches. |
| **"You may transfer, sell, or gift."** | Full resale rights via the Source Ledger provenance chain. |
| **"If we cease to exist, everything becomes public domain."** | The Dead Man's Repair Clause ensures perpetual access. |
| **"Repair voids nothing."** | Warranty remains intact regardless of who performs repairs or which parts are used. |

## 9. The Ward Checkpoint for Repair

Every system, device, or policy within Noble Beacon must pass the **Repair Ward Checkpoint** — six questions that identify hidden repair restrictions:

1. **Can the steward open it?** If disassembly requires proprietary tools or destructive force, it fails.
2. **Can the steward understand it?** If documentation is missing, obfuscated, or paywalled, it fails.
3. **Can the steward source parts?** If components are serialized, paired, or sole-sourced from the manufacturer, it fails.
4. **Can the steward fix it alone?** If repair requires internet connectivity, manufacturer authorization, or specialized certification, it fails.
5. **Can the steward pass it forward?** If the device cannot be transferred, gifted, or inherited with full functionality, it fails.
6. **Can the steward refuse changes?** If the device can be remotely updated, degraded, or disabled without consent, it fails.

Any system that fails even one checkpoint is **rejected from the archive** until the restriction is removed.
